MOHELA Student Loan Refinancing Rates and Terms

Home / Blog / Blog Details

Student loan debt remains a pressing issue for millions of Americans, with the total outstanding balance surpassing $1.7 trillion. As borrowers navigate repayment options, refinancing has emerged as a popular strategy to secure lower interest rates and better terms. MOHELA (the Missouri Higher Education Loan Authority) is one of the largest federal student loan servicers, but it also offers refinancing options for private loans. Here’s a deep dive into MOHELA’s refinancing rates and terms—and how they stack up in today’s economic climate.

Why Refinancing Student Loans Matters Now

With inflation cooling but interest rates still elevated, many borrowers are reevaluating their student loan strategies. The Federal Reserve’s rate hikes over the past two years have made borrowing more expensive, but refinancing can still provide relief—especially for those with high-interest private loans.

The Impact of Rising Interest Rates

Since 2022, the Fed’s aggressive monetary policy has pushed interest rates to their highest levels in decades. While federal loan rates are fixed, private loan borrowers may face variable rates that fluctuate with market conditions. Refinancing to a fixed-rate loan through MOHELA could provide stability in an uncertain economy.

Post-Pandemic Repayment Challenges

The end of the federal student loan payment pause in 2023 left many borrowers scrambling to adjust their budgets. Those with private loans never benefited from the pause, making refinancing an attractive option to reduce monthly payments.

MOHELA’s Refinancing Options

MOHELA offers both fixed and variable-rate refinancing options, catering to different borrower needs. Here’s a breakdown of their current offerings:

Fixed-Rate Refinancing

Fixed-rate loans provide predictability, locking in an interest rate for the life of the loan. As of mid-2024, MOHELA’s fixed rates range from 5.49% to 8.99% APR, depending on creditworthiness and loan term.

Pros of Fixed-Rate Refinancing

  • No surprises—monthly payments stay the same.
  • Protection against future rate hikes.
  • Ideal for borrowers planning long-term repayment.

Cons of Fixed-Rate Refinancing

  • Initial rates may be higher than variable options.
  • Less flexibility if market rates drop significantly.

Variable-Rate Refinancing

Variable-rate loans often start with lower APRs but can adjust over time based on market conditions. MOHELA’s variable rates currently range from 4.99% to 8.49% APR.

Pros of Variable-Rate Refinancing

  • Lower initial rates compared to fixed loans.
  • Potential savings if interest rates decline.

Cons of Variable-Rate Refinancing

  • Monthly payments can increase if rates rise.
  • Less budget stability for risk-averse borrowers.

Eligibility and Application Process

Not everyone qualifies for MOHELA’s refinancing program. Here’s what borrowers need to know before applying.

Credit and Income Requirements

MOHELA typically requires:
- A minimum credit score of 650 (though 700+ is preferred for the best rates).
- Stable income to demonstrate repayment ability.
- A debt-to-income ratio below 50%.

Loan Terms and Repayment Options

Borrowers can choose repayment terms ranging from 5 to 20 years, with longer terms resulting in lower monthly payments but higher total interest costs. MOHELA also offers:
- Auto-pay discounts (typically 0.25% off the APR).
- No prepayment penalties, allowing borrowers to pay off loans early without fees.

How MOHELA Compares to Other Lenders

MOHELA isn’t the only player in student loan refinancing. Competitors like SoFi, Earnest, and Laurel Road often advertise lower rates, but MOHELA’s strong customer service and nonprofit status appeal to some borrowers.

Key Differences

  • SoFi: Often has slightly lower rates but stricter credit requirements.
  • Earnest: Uses a unique underwriting model that considers savings and earning potential.
  • Laurel Road: Offers specialized discounts for healthcare professionals.

Should You Refinance with MOHELA?

Refinancing isn’t the right move for everyone. Borrowers with federal loans should weigh the pros and cons carefully, as refinancing forfeits federal protections like income-driven repayment and loan forgiveness.

When Refinancing Makes Sense

  • You have high-interest private loans.
  • Your credit score has improved since taking out the original loan.
  • You want to simplify payments by consolidating multiple loans.

When to Keep Federal Loans

  • You’re pursuing Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF).
  • You rely on income-driven repayment plans.
  • You anticipate needing federal deferment or forbearance options.

Final Tips for Refinancing Success

Before committing to MOHELA or any lender, borrowers should:
1. Shop around—compare rates from at least three lenders.
2. Check for discounts—some lenders offer perks for alumni or professional associations.
3. Read the fine print—understand fees, rate adjustment terms, and repayment flexibility.

With careful research, refinancing through MOHELA could be a smart financial move—but it’s crucial to make an informed decision based on individual circumstances.
